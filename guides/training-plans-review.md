# Training Plans — Comparative Review & Ranking

**Athlete profile (from the documents):** Male · 29 yrs · 5'6" · 71 kg · BMI 22.7 · ~18–19% BF · Hyderabad hostel · daily 40-min swim · morning training window.

**Reviewer's summary:** All four documents are well-structured and draw from solid sports-science principles (progressive overload, recomposition, MEV/MAV/MRV volume landmarks, deload periodisation, Hyderabad-friendly nutrition). But they are **not equivalent** — they differ on two critical axes: **(1) assumed training age** and **(2) how honestly they handle the swim + hostel recovery constraint**. Those two differences decide which plan actually works for you on Day 1.

## 1. `elite-performance-system-v6.html` — The Complete Human Performance System (V6)

### Score: 9.4 / 10 🥇

### What makes it the best overall document
- **24-month horizon, not just 16 weeks.** Five phases from Movement Foundation (Weeks 1–4) to Elite Athletic Performance (Months 17–24). This matches physiology — aesthetic physiques take 18–24 months, not 16 weeks.
- **Honest self-correction.** The document explicitly *names and fixes* the flaws in the V3 plan: V3's 2400 kcal flat was too low for 6-day training, V3's deload at Week 6 was too late, V3's 130–140 kg deadlift target was fantasy. That meta-awareness is rare and valuable.
- **Cites actual literature.** Schoenfeld 2017, Israetel 2019, Longland 2016 recomposition study, Aragon & Schoenfeld 2013 on the post-workout window. You can audit the claims.
- **Phased split structure.** Starts with a 4-day Upper/Lower (Weeks 1–4), then progresses to 6-day PPL A/B (Weeks 5–12). This is what connective tissue actually needs.
- **Fatigue management framework.** Distinguishes local fatigue vs systemic (CNS) fatigue. Gives a five-signal athlete dashboard (strength drop, RHR, sleep, mood, joint discomfort) with explicit deload triggers.
- **Adaptive Training System (Section 21).** Turns the plan into a feedback loop — five input signals, a rule table, Sunday auto-review. This is the section that separates a document from a coach.
- **Aesthetic Proportion Model.** Ratios (shoulder:waist 1.60–1.65, chest:waist 1.40, arm:neck 1.0) anchor *why* certain exercises are in the plan.
- **Calibrated strength projections.** Conservative Week 16 targets — Bench 72.5 kg × 5, Squat 95 kg × 5, Deadlift 112.5 kg × 3 — explicitly replacing V3's over-optimistic targets.

### What it does less well
- Dense and academic. It reads like a training manual, not a daily playbook. Harder to open at 6 AM and execute.
- Phase 1 details (the 4-day Upper/Lower) are thinner than Phase 2 — you need to cross-reference the six-month-program for richer Week-1 detail.
- No truly day-by-day meal breakdown the way the athlete training manual has.
- Section 18–20 (Auto Body Analysis, Physique Scoring, Strength Prediction) are interesting but won't drive Day-1 adherence.

### Suggested improvements
1. **Add a "Monday morning checklist"** to Section 9 — something you can photograph and tape to a gym wall (sets/reps/rest/RPE for Week 1 Upper A, no prose).
2. **Expand Phase 1** — give the same exercise-level depth to Weeks 1–4 that Phase 2 has. Currently the 4-day split is a table; it should be a session walkthrough.
3. **Add a "swim integration" section** as comprehensive as the one in the Athlete Training Manual and the Six-Month Program — V6's treatment of swim is thinner than the others and that's the athlete's biggest recovery variable.
4. **Combine Sections 18–20 into a single "Quarterly Self-Audit" section** — they overlap significantly.
5. **Include a deload week sample session**, not just a parameter table.

## 2. `six-month-program.html` — The Beginner's Covenant

### Score: 9.2 / 10 🥈 · **👉 START HERE**

### Why this is the one to start this week
- **Correctly assumes zero gym training age.** Everything from 2-month history to "low training age" in other documents is generous — in reality, no programme has been run end-to-end, and connective tissue has never been loaded. This plan treats that as the truth.
- **The only plan that honestly addresses the swim + gym shoulder load problem.** Section 3 explicitly eliminates the 6-day PPL for the first 24 weeks because: 6 gym + 7 swim = 13 weekly sessions; anterior deltoid is hit 9× per week counting freestyle; rotator cuff impingement becomes likely by Week 6–8. No other file does this math.
- **Progresses 4-day → 5-day (not 4-day → 6-day).** Keeps two complete gym-off days (Thursday and Sunday) alongside the daily swim. This is safer and more sustainable than the 6-day PPL found in Files 1 and 3.
- **The Beginner's Rules (Section 2).** Seven rules — form before weight, progressive overload, recovery is training, 7-hour sleep floor, deliberate hydration, patience over panic, consistency is the whole game. These are the rules most beginners *don't* know on Day 1 and they're the difference between a 24-week finish and a Week-5 quit.
- **Honest expectations.** −3 to −4% body fat, +3 to +4 kg lean mass, waist −2 to −3 cm over 24 weeks. No fantasy 140 kg deadlift in 16 weeks.
- **Habit Blueprint (Section 11).** The Identity Shift framing ("I train" vs "I'm trying to get fit"), Week-by-week psychology (Doubt Zone at Week 3–6 is the quitting window), Seven Rules for Making This Permanent, Emergency Protocol for when motivation dies. This is the section that makes it work at Month 6 instead of Month 3.
- **14-day immediate checklist at the end.** Day 1 measurements, Day 2 grocery run, Day 3 print the schedule — operational, not aspirational.

### What it does less well
- Phase 3 is less detailed than Phases 1 and 2 — one sample Push session is shown, the rest is implied.
- No adaptive feedback system like V6's Section 21.
- No strength prediction model or physique scoring.
- Stops at Week 24 — needs pairing with V6 for the continuation.

### Suggested improvements
1. **Add V6's adaptive review rules** (Section 21 of V6) as a Week-9 onboarding. Before Week 9 the plan runs as written; from Week 9 the Sunday review starts.
2. **Expand Phase 3** to full session detail — Pull, Legs, Upper, Lower should each get the same 7-exercise detail the sample Push gets.
3. **Add a "Return from missed week" protocol** — if you miss 5+ days due to illness or travel, what do you do? Currently implied, not specified.
4. **Add body measurement photos template** — mark the tape positions on a silhouette diagram for consistency across 24 weeks.
5. **One-page "Phase 1 cheat sheet"** — Monday through Friday, each session on one line, laminated-and-bag-pocket format.

## 3. `elite-athlete-training-manual.html` — 6-Day PPL + Swim System

### Score: 8.3 / 10 🥉

### Strengths — this file has the best exercise-level detail of all four
- **The Full Body Coverage table is the best in any of the four documents.** 17 muscle groups mapped against 6 training days with weekly set counts. No muscle is left unaudited.
- **Exercise-by-exercise coaching notes are excellent.** Every lift has bar path, RPE, rest, cue, *why-it-matters* reasoning — e.g. Bulgarian split squat as "most humbling exercise", straight-arm pulldown as "pure lat, zero biceps", farmer's carries as "most underrated strength + conditioning". These cues are the difference between moving weight and training.
- **Day-by-day swim prescription.** Push A = leg-driven freestyle. Push B = breaststroke + backstroke (no overhead stress). Legs A = pull-buoy (legs off). Pull B = backstroke-heavy (spinal decompression post-deadlift). This is the most sophisticated swim integration of the four.
- **Weekly muscle volume distribution is scientifically sound.** Chest 16–18 sets, back 14–16 sets, shoulders 14+ sets — sits cleanly in the MAV range for an intermediate natural.
- **6-meal nutrition day is operationally complete.** Each meal has grams, kcal, and macros down to the gram.
- **Sunday Reset Day protocol.** 10 concrete recovery actions from sleep to contrast shower to 4-7-8 breathing.

### Where it falls short
- **Assumes an intermediate you may not be yet.** "You've trained consistently for 2 months" is the opening premise. If that's generous, the 6-day PPL from Day 1 is a shoulder impingement risk, especially stacked with 6× weekly 30–40 min swims.
- **No Phase 1 foundation.** Drops you straight into a 6-day PPL. No 4-week technique audit. V6 and the Six-Month Program both correctly insert that foundation first.
- **Deload placement.** Week 5, 10, 15 — correct timing, but less explicit trigger rules than V6.
- **No Phase 4 / beyond-Week-16.** Like V3, this ends at Week 16 and leaves the next 22 months of your lifting life unwritten.
- **Strength projections (Week 16: +20 kg bench, +30 kg squat, +35 kg deadlift) are optimistic** for a true beginner — closer to what an early intermediate achieves.
- Slightly over-aspirational language in places ("Greek god chest", "yoke look") — fine if it motivates, risky if it drives ego lifting.

### Suggested improvements
1. **Insert a 4–8 week Phase 0 Foundation** (4-day Upper/Lower) before the 6-day PPL begins. Steal it from either V6 Phase 1 or Six-Month Program Phase 1.
2. **Make the 2-month training prerequisite explicit at the top.** If the athlete hasn't completed a structured programme end-to-end, send them to Phase 0 first — put that decision tree on page 1.
3. **Add the Adaptive Rules table from V6 Section 21** — this document has the best daily detail but the weakest feedback loop.
4. **Calibrate Week 16 strength targets downward** by 15–20% to match V6's more realistic projections.
5. **Add "when to skip the swim" criteria** — currently the swim is presumed. On a day with < 6 hours sleep, or after a Week 5 deload, or with a new joint ache, the right answer might be walk 20 min instead.

## 4. `elite-plan-final-v3.html` — The 71kg Transformation Plan V3

### Score: 7.0 / 10

### Why this ranks last — it's the earliest draft that V6 was written to fix

This isn't a bad document; it's just been explicitly superseded. V6 says so out loud. The issues below are the reasons you should not use V3 as your operating document.

### Strengths
- **Good "Coach's Verdict" opening.** The Recomposition rationale (not-too-fat-for-bulk, not-too-skinny-for-cut) is clearly argued.
- **Day-specific diets** (Push day / Pull day / Leg day / Rest day have slightly different macros). More granular than most plans.
- **Beginner Progression Roadmap tab** is actually good — Phase 0 (Weeks 1–6) Foundation before full PPL. This should have been the headline, not a tab.
- **Elite Upgrades section** (upper chest priority, power work, grip/neck training) is a useful *add-on* — but it's placed as a "10/10 upgrade" rather than optional.
- **Has a 10-mistakes list and an Ab Visibility reality check** (14–15% BF for visible abs) which is honest.

### Where it falls short — the documented flaws
- **Flat 2400 kcal target.** V6 correctly identifies this as creating a ~500 kcal daily deficit during 6-day high-volume training, which blunts muscle growth. The correct structure is 2500–2550 on training days, 2200 on rest — a difference V6 explicitly fixes.
- **Week 16 strength targets are fantasy for a beginner.** Bench 75–80 × 5, Squat 100–105 × 5, Deadlift 130–140 × 3 at 71 kg BW in 16 weeks natural is top 5% outcomes, not "expected." V6 explicitly corrects this: Bench 72.5, Squat 95, Deadlift 112.5 is the honest number.
- **Deload at Week 6 and 12 is too late** for a 6-day split. Week 5 / 10 / 15 (V6 and the others) is correct.
- **Straight to 6-day PPL with 2 hours/day.** For a hostel-based natural who hasn't finished a programme end-to-end, this is a recipe for overtraining within 8 weeks. The beginner Foundation phase exists in the document but it's filed as an afterthought.
- **Deadlift placement (Friday) on a CNS already fatigued by Wednesday squat.** V6 explicitly moves deadlift to Monday to open the week on a fresh CNS.
- **Back volume (~34 sets/week) sits above MRV.** V6 caps back at 16–18 sets; more is not better once MAV is crossed.
- **The "Elite Upgrades" framing** encourages adding power work, neck training, and grip before the base is built. For a beginner, these are premature.
- **Tone is more motivational than coaching** — "Most brutal day", "King of all exercises", "Greek god chest" — feels like a pitch. V6 and Six-Month are coaches; V3 is a copywriter.

### Suggested improvements
1. **Merge with V6.** Honestly — V6 is the corrected version of this document. Use V6 instead, archive V3.
2. If keeping V3 alive: **make the Beginner Roadmap the main plan**, not a tab. 6–8 weeks Foundation, then graduate to PPL.
3. **Recalibrate macros** to V6's 2500/2200 split.
4. **Recalibrate strength targets** to V6's conservative projections.
5. **Move deadlift to Monday**, squat to Wednesday, drop weekly back volume to 16–18 working sets.
6. **Remove the "Elite Upgrades 10/10" framing.** These are Month-9 additions, not Week-5 additions.

## Cross-Cutting Improvements (apply to all four)

These are gaps I'd recommend fixing in whichever plan you adopt:

### 1. Hostel-specific sleep protocol
All four plans say "7+ hours" but don't seriously address shared dormitories. Add: eye mask (₹80), foam earplugs (₹40 pack of 10), phone charging on the other side of the room, blackout curtain if possible, white noise app (free). Sleep is more actionable with gear than with willpower.

### 2. Injury decision tree
"Stop if you feel pain" is everywhere but no plan tells you *what to do next*. A simple flowchart — muscle soreness (continue), joint pain under load (stop set, drop weight 20%, test), sharp pain (end session, 48 hours off, reassess), pain that persists > 5 days (see physiotherapist) — belongs in all four.

### 3. Travel / disrupted week protocol
Every plan has a Sunday rest day; none has a "you're travelling for 5 days with no gym" playbook. A 20-minute bodyweight circuit (push-ups, goblet squat with backpack, inverted rows under a desk, walking lunges, plank) preserves the habit when the infrastructure is gone.

### 4. Progress photo discipline
Same phone, same time of day, same light, same angle, same t-shirt or shirtless — all four mention photos, none gives the exact protocol. A 5-line checklist prevents the "I look the same" illusion that's caused by inconsistent lighting.

### 5. Vegetarian fallback menus
Three of four plans lean on chicken. If a hostel day goes meat-free (common in Hyderabad messes), a one-to-one substitution table — 150 g chicken = 200 g paneer OR 50 g soy chunks (dry) + 2 eggs OR 1 scoop whey + 100 g tofu — keeps protein hit without thinking.

### 6. Creatine specifics
All four recommend creatine; none specifies micronized monohydrate (the form with evidence), no loading phase needed, 5 g in any liquid, take with a meal for GI tolerance if needed. One sentence prevents a month of the wrong product.

### 7. Morning weight + RHR as the real dashboard
All four mention tracking weight and RHR — but none gives the interpretation rules as a single chart. The V6 adaptive rules (Section 21) are the closest; lift them into whichever plan you run.

### 8. "When the plan is working" vs "when it's not" diagnostic
All four over-index on what to do when things go wrong. A parallel "signs the plan is working" checklist (strength up week-over-week on any two of five main lifts, waist flat or down, energy ≥ 7/10 average, sleep ≥ 7 hrs average, joints quiet) lets you confidently keep going when results feel invisible — which is the Week 3–6 Doubt Zone the Six-Month Program correctly identifies as the top quitting window.
