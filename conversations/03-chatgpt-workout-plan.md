Why PPL Over a "Body Part Split" (like Monday Chest, Tuesday Shoulders, etc.)?
A classic body part split (often called a "bro split") dedicates each day to 1-2 specific muscles, like:

Monday: Chest
Tuesday: Shoulders
Wednesday: Core
Thursday: Legs
Friday: Cardio
Saturday: Arms or Back, etc.

This can feel appealing because it gives "full focus" on one area, but for most beginners (and even intermediates), it's not the best choice. Here's a clear comparison:

Higher training frequency in PPL → Each major muscle group gets trained twice per week (e.g., chest/shoulders/triceps on both Push days). Research and real-world results show this leads to faster strength gains and muscle growth for beginners compared to hitting a muscle only once a week (like in a strict bro split). Muscles recover in 48-72 hours, so training them more often (but with good recovery) stimulates better progress without overdoing it.
Better balance and efficiency → PPL groups movements by function (push, pull, legs), which prevents imbalances (e.g., overdeveloping chest but neglecting back). It also allows compound exercises (big multi-muscle moves like presses, rows, squats) that build overall strength quicker. In a body part split, sessions can feel shorter on some days but less "full-body functional."
More volume per week without burnout → With 6 days, PPL repeats the cycle twice, giving solid weekly volume (sets/reps) per muscle. A body part split often spreads volume too thin—one intense chest day might leave you sore for days, but the muscle only gets hit once.
Beginner suitability → PPL (or similar higher-frequency splits) is widely recommended for new lifters over bro splits because it builds a strong base faster. Bro splits work great for advanced bodybuilders who need extreme isolation and recovery focus, but beginners progress better with more frequent practice on form and compounds.

If you did a body part split 6 days a week, you'd risk overtraining smaller groups (like shoulders or arms) or undertraining legs/core, and recovery might suffer since you're hitting the gym daily without full rest patterns.
That said, if you really prefer focusing on one area per day for motivation, we could tweak to an upper/lower or modified split—but for your 6-day setup as a beginner, PPL is superior for results.
For Six-Pack Abs: Do You Need Daily Core Work?
No, you don't need to train core/abs every day—in fact, for most people (especially beginners), it's unnecessary and can slow recovery or lead to poor form in other lifts.

Abs (rectus abdominis for the "six-pack" look) recover quickly like other muscles, but 2-4 times per week is plenty for growth and strength. Studies show no big extra benefit from daily training vs. 2-3x/week if total weekly volume (sets) is similar.
Visible six-pack secret → It's 80% diet/body fat reduction (you need low body fat ~10-15% for men to see abs), not endless crunches. Training builds the muscle underneath, but fat loss reveals it. Daily abs won't speed that up much and might cause overuse issues.
Best approach for you:

Keep sessions short: 3-4 exercises, 3 sets each, 10-20 reps (focus on controlled movement, not speed).
